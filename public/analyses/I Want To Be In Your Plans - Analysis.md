# "I Want To Be In Your Plans"

## Overview

"I Want To Be In Your Plans" is a long-distance plea written from the wrong side of a relationship's ending. The partner the speaker addresses has moved far away and is building a new life with someone else; what remains between them is conversation, memory, and the speaker's grief. Across four stanzas, the poem moves through paradox (still close, already lost), petition (asking to be allowed back in), accountability (naming what the speaker did wrong while the relationship was still alive), and finally surrender — a quiet, almost prayed bargain to be granted the only thing the speaker still wants. It is a poem about the strange shape of grief for someone who is alive, reachable, even friendly, but no longer yours.

## Thematic Analysis

### Closeness Without Reach

The opening stanza establishes the poem's central paradox. The speaker and the addressee are "still so close" — they speak, they share a "daily dose" of one another's voices — but the closeness is the kind built across distance, the kind that lives entirely in language. The partner has moved away, and physical intimacy has gone with the geography: "your touch now, I feel like I will no longer know." The "daily dose" image is ambivalent and a little wounded; conversation is medicine, but it's also the only thing left, and the speaker can feel it being administered rather than given. The relationship hasn't ended in the formal sense of contact ceasing. It has ended in the more devastating sense that the form of contact has been demoted from love to friendliness.

### The Plea and the Promise

The second stanza is the poem's only forward-looking gesture. "Take your time, but please come back to me" gives the partner room to be where she is and with whom she is, while still asking — quietly, insistently — for return. The promise that follows reverses the wound that likely caused the original break: "When I can, I promise to move anywhere with you." Geography is named directly here. The partner moved; the speaker did not, or could not. The line "When I can" is honest about a present limitation rather than offering a fantasy. The promise isn't that the speaker will follow tomorrow; it's that whenever the speaker is able, the answer will be yes. This reads as a correction of an earlier refusal — an attempt to rewrite the moment that may have ended things.

### Accountability Without Self-Pity

The third stanza is the poem's moral center. The speaker names their failures plainly: "I'm forever sorry for what we've both done / I take accountability for my part." The phrase "both done" preserves shared responsibility without using it as cover. The line "since my nighttime became difficult" is one of the poem's most carefully veiled lines, gesturing toward depression, insomnia, or some private suffering that pulled the speaker inward when the partner needed them outward. It explains without excusing. The sun metaphor — "place you like my sun" — is rueful and past-tense; this is the love the speaker should have given, framed as the gravitational center the partner deserved to be. The closing line of the stanza, about forgetting "all the times you were fun" during arguments, captures the small, ordinary erasure of recent conflict crowding out a long, good history. By the time the speaker remembered, she was already gone.

### Living as a Widower

The fourth stanza abandons hope's tentative gestures and settles into present reality. The anaphoric "And now I live with these regrets / And now I live like a widower" is the poem's emotional climax. The widower image is precise and unsparing. A widower mourns someone whose life ended; the speaker mourns someone whose life simply continued without him. The partner is alive, reachable, even still in conversation — but her romantic life has gone on, has chosen someone else, and the speaker is left performing the rituals of grief while she builds a future on the other side of the country, the world, the relationship. The closing couplet shifts register entirely. "If only God could give me the only thing I want, I would forever stay your man" turns the poem into a kind of prayer-bargain. The plural "plans" of the title becomes a singular wish, and the wish is structured as devotion: not a demand, but an offer — constancy in exchange for the chance to be hers again.

## Literary Devices

### Paradox as Foundation

The poem is built on contradictions held in tension: still close yet long lost, talking constantly yet losing touch, accountable yet still pleading, alive yet widowed. These paradoxes don't resolve — they coexist, mirroring the lived experience of a love that ended without ending cleanly.

### Anaphora as Resignation

The repetition of "And now I live" in the final stanza enacts the slow accumulation of grief. The construction stacks the speaker's new states of being — regret, widowhood, longing — into a single weighted present tense. Repetition becomes the formal equivalent of inertia, of a life that keeps going even after the part of it that mattered has stopped.

### Astronomical Imagery

The "sun" in "place you like my sun" makes the partner the gravitational center of the speaker's emotional cosmos. The image is unspoken in the rest of the poem but informs the widower metaphor: when the sun goes out — or, in this case, rises somewhere else — the world doesn't end, but it goes dark. The poem implies a speaker still orbiting an absent center.

### The Bargain Structure

The closing line transforms the poem into a transactional prayer: *if* God grants this, *then* I will give that. The structure is theological, almost vow-like, and elevates the romantic plea into something approaching the sacred. The simplicity of "the only thing I want" insists that the rest of life — ambition, geography, even faith — has been reduced to this single asking.

## Line-by-Line Interpretation

**First stanza:** The opening lines establish that two people can remain in each other's daily orbit while the love between them has already ended. "Daily dose" suggests a dependency on conversation as a substitute for what's been lost — sustenance, but not the thing itself. The line "your touch now, I feel like I will no longer know" is the poem's first direct admission that physical intimacy has ended, both because of distance and because she is now someone else's to touch.

**Second stanza:** The plea to be given time is more selfless than it appears. The speaker isn't asking for instant reconciliation; they're asking only for the chance of return. The promise of geographic flexibility — "move anywhere with you" — directly addresses what is almost certainly the original injury: the partner moved, the speaker stayed, and somewhere in that asymmetry the relationship cracked. The closing line, "I only wish all this desire could come true," sits at the edge of resignation; desire and possibility have been quietly separated.

**Third stanza:** The phrase "since my nighttime became difficult" is the poem's most carefully veiled confession. It hints at depression, sleeplessness, the shape of a private suffering that pulled the speaker inward and away from the partner who was already, perhaps, considering leaving. The acknowledgment that arguments made the speaker forget "all the times you were fun" reveals an awareness of how conflict erases context — how the recent and painful crowds out the long and good, until the relationship looks, in the moment, like only its worst version.

**Fourth stanza:** "I live like a widower" is the poem's central image and its sharpest grief, made sharper by the fact that the partner is alive and well and choosing someone else. The widower mourns someone who once shared a life; the speaker mourns someone who is sharing one now, just not with him. The final line's bargain — "I would forever stay your man" — refuses the typical posture of love poems addressed to the lost. There's no claim that the speaker deserves this, no insistence on their worth. There's only the offer of constancy, traded for the gift of being chosen again.

## Connection to the Collection

The poem's most immediate kin is "May Things Go Well For You," another address to a partner who has moved on. Both poems share direct address ("Hey, honey" / "please come back to me") and the recognition that the speaker contributed to the ending. But where "May Things Go Well For You" has reached a kind of release — wishing the beloved well, letting her go — this poem hasn't gotten there yet. The speaker is still in the bargaining stage, still hoping return is possible, still offering trades to a God who hasn't answered.

The "nighttime became difficult" line connects the poem to the broader mental-health currents in "I Woke Up Low Again (August 5th)," "Drone Days," "Blackwell's Light," and "How The Little Boy Drowned." Across the collection, the speaker's depression isn't presented as a discrete topic but as an undertow that pulls at every other relationship — to lovers, to friends, to the self. Here, that undertow is named as the specific force that pulled the speaker away from a partner who needed presence. The accountability the speaker takes for letting the darkness eclipse the love is one of the most honest moments in the collection.

The widower metaphor also echoes the survivor language of "How The Little Boy Drowned." In both poems, the speaker is left living after a kind of death — once metaphorically his own, here metaphorically the relationship's — and asked to perform the work of carrying on while grieving.

## Emotional Resonance

The poem's quiet power lies in its restraint. The speaker doesn't blame the partner for moving, doesn't resent the new life she's building, doesn't claim victimhood. They name what they did wrong, ask for time, and offer what they still have to offer — willingness, devotion, the readiness to follow now that they refused to follow before. The widower image is the poem's most unforgettable gesture, capturing the strange shape of grief for someone still alive, still reachable, still fond, but no longer yours. The closing prayer-bargain refuses both bitterness and easy hope; it simply asks. There is something deeply human in a plea that knows it may not be answered and offers itself anyway. The poem doesn't resolve the situation it describes. It records, with painful clarity, the speaker's last honest attempt to remain.
